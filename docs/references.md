# Reading List

For those interested in the academic foundations behind this work. These are not implementation guides — they are the research that shaped my thinking about how to apply machine learning to markets honestly.

## Quantitative Finance & ML

**López de Prado, M. (2018).** *Advances in Financial Machine Learning.* Wiley.

The most important book written on the intersection of ML and finance. Identifies the structural flaws in standard backtesting methodology and proposes rigorous alternatives. Essential reading for anyone applying machine learning to markets.

**López de Prado, M. (2020).** *Machine Learning for Asset Managers.* Cambridge University Press.

A more concise treatment focused on portfolio construction and the practical application of ML techniques to asset management.

## Statistical Validation

**Bailey, D. H., & López de Prado, M. (2014).** The Deflated Sharpe Ratio: Correcting for Selection Bias, Backtest Overfitting and Non-Normality. *Journal of Portfolio Management*, 40(5), 94–107.

Introduces the Deflated Sharpe Ratio — the statistical test that separates genuine strategy performance from the expected best result of random trials. If you read one paper on backtest evaluation, make it this one.

**Opdyke, J. D. (2007).** Comparing Sharpe Ratios: So Where Are the p-values? *Journal of Asset Management*, 8(5), 308–336.

Derives the distribution of the Sharpe Ratio estimator under non-normal returns. The mathematical foundation for understanding when a Sharpe Ratio is statistically meaningful.

## Volatility & Econometrics

**Engle, R. F. (1982).** Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity with Estimates of the Variance of United Kingdom Inflation. *Econometrica*, 50(4), 987–1007.

Nobel Prize-winning work establishing that volatility itself is predictable and time-varying. Foundational for any strategy that adapts to changing market conditions.

## Complexity Science

**Mitchell, M. (2009).** *Complexity: A Guided Tour.* Oxford University Press.

An accessible introduction to complexity science — emergence, adaptation, and the behaviour of systems where the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. The conceptual framework I apply to understanding markets.

**Holland, J. H. (1995).** *Hidden Order: How Adaptation Builds Complexity.* Addison-Wesley.

On adaptive systems and how complex behaviour emerges from simple rules — directly relevant to why markets behave the way they do, and why ML models trained on market data can exhibit emergent intelligence.
